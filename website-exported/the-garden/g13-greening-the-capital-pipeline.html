Putting it All Together: How SPARCC Partners Connected Technical Assistance and Flexible Grant Funding to Build Community Climate Resilience and Wealth

By Mona Wang

“Turn it off!” I grew up with a dad that shouted after us any time we left a room with the light still on. As immigrants, every penny counted. Same with the TV and all appliances at home. Heating was a luxury reserved only for when our entire wardrobe couldn’t cut it anymore. Otherwise, it was a waste of energy, but mostly, money. And every appliance turned off and unplugged when not in use, was money saved, sometimes at the cost of comfort, but it was something we got used to.

But what if there were a way to save hundreds, even thousands of dollars without having to compromise comfort – especially for low-income families and small businesses of color who already work so hard to make ends meet, at a time when having the means to be able to handle extreme weather shifts could make the difference between resilience or displacement?

Climate change stands to hurt low-income communities and communities of color more, because these communities are already faced with the challenges of making do with less. Just one extreme weather event, flash flood, or natural disaster is enough to risk pushing these families and communities over the edge, let alone having to deal with recurring events like these that return year after year in increasing magnitudes. This disparity also creates drastic consequences for the health of communities. Studies have shown that both mental and physical health are negatively affected by increased climate vulnerability, given the increased exposure to health risks coupled with the rising costs for care. As a whole, this means that living with climate change costs more for low-income and communities of color even though they’ve contributed the least to the state of climate crisis we currently face.

These disproportionalities are why SPARCC worked through a trio of lenses: climate, health, and racial equity. SPARCC understood that the climate work could not be separate from the health or racial equity work. The same with capital, although it didn’t start out that way. By the second phase of the initiative, we realized the importance of integrating climate resilience into our capital strategy and dedicated a portion of its $3MM in capital grants to fund the upfront costs for projects within its capital pipeline to afford energy efficiency, water efficiency, health, and climate resilience upgrades.

Now as the initiative draws to a close, we can proudly say that SPARCC strengthened the climate resilience in all six SPARCC sites of Atlanta, Bay Area, Chicago, Denver, Los Angeles, and Memphis through its technical assistance and capital grants as well through other grant fund sources.

The impact was that community leaders were able to strengthen community climate resilience and thereby reduce the pressures of displacement due to climate change, save thousands of dollars, improve their health, and reduce their carbon footprint.

Lessons Learned

Reaching this success came with a great deal of lessons along the way that we hope can be useful for other practitioners, community development financial institutions (CDFIs), and policymakers so that they won’t have to go through the same trial and error that we went through, but mostly so that they can continue to build on these lessons to create more support and unlock even greater opportunity. Here are the most important lessons we believe were critical to the success of building climate resilience from the ground up:

1. Small grant funds have an outsized impact. Dedicating grant funds early in the development process to provide locally-appropriate technical assistance is key to unlocking energy efficiency, water efficiency, health, and other resilience benefits. Communities are interested in health and climate resilience measures, but in most cases, lack the time and access to technical experts and, most of all, funds to take on these endeavors. SPARCC discovered that connecting community partners with technical experts locally and within the community was oftentimes all that was needed to get a project’s final building design to integrate the most resilient features. The recommendations from energy and resilience assessments were able to be integrated into the final designs at little to no additional cost to the owner and residents. These costs were simply absorbed into the existing project budgets, due to a number of different factors, including:

  • Sometimes swapping out the older, less efficient parts of a building design (I.e., gas) with higher efficiency parts (I.e., electric) costs just about the same as buying new versions of the older, less efficient models. For example, the cost of setting up a building to go all-electric just about evened out with the cost of having to purchase new gas-powered appliances and the gas infrastructure, such as piping, required with it, which meant a cheaper energy alternative (electric) at about the same cost.

  • the project budgets simply came with enough additional room for small but impactful resilience upgrades.

Not only were the health and climate resilience measures able to be integrated into the final project designs at little to no upfront costs, but these efficiencies and improvements will continue to save owners and residents money for decades to come thanks to reduced utilities bills.

2. CDFIs have access to more funding resources. While grant funding for resilience and health upgrades can be combined with public sources, funding and financing at the CDFI level makes it easier for smaller owners and developers that don't always qualify for energy/climate programs.

3. Start early and it will pay for itself. In the long-term, the inclusion of climate resiliency and energy efficiency in a project’s design and scoping will likely reduce costs and pay for itself. Addressing the funding gap for technical assistance and upfront installation costs is critical to ensuring that low-income communities and communities of color are climate resilient and that projects financed by CDFIs and other intermediaries have a better chance of withstanding the extremes and uncertainties of a changing climate.

4. Be flexible. With flexible funds, we’ve been able to enhance the reach and impact of capital grants that allow for the inclusion of climate resilience and energy efficiency experts early.

Example Projects

Latinos Progresando Electrification and More. An anchor institution for immigration, culture, and community services in Chicago’s southwest side, Latinos Progresando became the first community organization to become all-electric in Chicago as a result of the SPARCC capital strategy. The model included a collaboration between Latinos Progresando (project developer), IFF (project designer and advocate), Elevate (energy TA) and SPARCC Capital (subsidy). The energy assessment yielded the integration of a heat pump HVAC system, enhanced window glazing, improved roof insulation, and COVID-safe rated air filtration. The project is projected to save $18,000 and 55 tons of GHG - which is the equivalent of the energy savings from planting 1729 tree seedlings grown for 10 years or taking almost 23 cars off the road.

Denver Solar-ready ADU Pilot. In Denver, the West Denver Renaissance Collaborative and Denver Housing are preparing to scale and increase the production of ADU’s through city-level rezoning and actions to reduce barriers to ADU development. Through partnering with Energy Outreach Colorado, the project is creating an ADU-specific solar-readiness checklist and recommendations for integrating energy efficiency, solar, and EV charging for new ADUs. These will be standardized to the design of the 6 proposed ADU models in all future builds. The recommendations will also be implemented in 5 pilot projects. The project will provide a model for addressing both displacement and demonstrating the benefits of climate-readiness for low and moderate income homeowners for the city. The projections expect that solar will offset 60-104% of energy costs in the ADU designs.

BuCu West Energy Efficiency. Also in Denver, the BuCu West development successfully applied for the Non Profit Energy Efficiency Program that Energy Outreach Colorado administers. They will receive public funds for technical assessment, materials and construction for integrating high efficiency LED lighting and attic insulation for the Morrison avenue project. As the program only covered a portion of the funds, SPARCC capital was able to provide the gap funding to make the improvements possible. As a result, the project expects to save 132 tons of GHG and $15,000 in operations savings.

Homan Square Permanent Supportive and Resilient Housing. The Homan Square project is providing new supportive and affordable housing and social cohesion for the community. To support climate resilience in this project, Enterprise Community Partners used the Enterprise Green Communities criteria to provide targeted review of high impact, low cost recommendations for the project team. The concept included a review of sustainability measures identified by the project team that were likely to be “value engineered” out of the final design due to cost barriers. The recommendations centered on the value of addressing health-equity impacts, such as improved air quality and respiratory health, healthy building materials, and addressing mental health and feelings of belonging through approachable design and way-finding.

Imani Village Development. The Chicago SPARCC table approached the SPARCC national team to integrate the TA model into Elevated Chicago’s work. Elevate is providing energy assessment, recommendations, and the identification of non-SPARCC funding for implementation. The current recommendations center on including solar and storage as part of the development, turning a portion of the community-accessible building into a resilience hub that can be accessed in the case of climate impacts or energy outages. The project will likely access state and local public funding sources, and anticipate that the solar development will offset a majority of cost outlays.

Community-driven capital investments can go a long way in low-income communities and communities of color in changing the course of historic racist policies, but they can go even farther when used to support community health and climate resilience. With the barriers of cost and technical capacity addressed, SPARCC helped strengthened its community partners’ ability to mitigate, withstand, and rebound from the impacts of climate change, including displacement–in a way that will continue to save these families and small businesses money long after the end of the initiative. Incorporating energy efficiency, health, and climate resilience into capital projects resulted in saving community partners thousands of dollars, much more than the pennies we used to save growing up. These savings can help businesses and families better handle climate and financial shocks and stressors, save more money, and ultimately contribute to building wealth. This is why we need more initiatives, focus, funds, and financing mechanisms to provide these communities with the opportunity to access these resilience measures.
