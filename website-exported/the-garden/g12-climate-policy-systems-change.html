Climate, Policy, and Systems Change

By Marissa Ramirez

In addition to supporting projects from the ground up, SPARCC aims to realize systemic change in our policies for a more just and equitable future. SPARCC’s collective insights are codified from community partners and local leaders in Pathways to Prosperity. The document was created to represent the collective work of partners and the initiative and serve as a blueprint for advocacy and policy implementation. The platform is intersectional - centering race, health equity, and climate justice.

Parks, Urban, and Open Space Equity Working Group

The Parks, Urban, and Open Space Equity Working Group came together in the first phase of SPARCC to address a shared challenge of displacement from large-scale green infrastructure development, developing a set of principles for Park Equity. Building on broad interest across the initiative, the group hosted a Virtual Town hall, hosted by Hannah Hamdi to approaches and solutions to green gentrification across the country as part of building whole and healthy neighborhoods. Building on these learnings, the group hosted a workshop on green infrastructure, flooding and racism in Atlanta. Partners shared and uplifted the community and government leaders to work together on these intersectional issues to support stormwater project implementation, long term funding and operations support, and metrics and accountability for hiring, contracting and procurement, especially on public property. Key project examples included Climate and Cultural Resilience Projects in Atlanta and Chicago, the Atlanta CREW project led by Southface and WAWA, and Partnership for Southern Equity’s community engagement framework.

Shared learnings on policy approaches and project examples became the framework for the working group’s evolution to lead the development of the Parks platform. The Platform, which is part of SPARCC’s Pathways to Prosperity, addresses the racist and classist attitudes of park-development. The platform prioritizes community-led park development, equitable park systems, and green investments for resilient neighborhoods.

Climate Leaders Network

As a collective, SPARCC identified a need for local climate leaders to play a bigger role in guiding and developing equitable and resilient outcomes in the communities we work. Climate leaders at the table offer a range of expertise, from technical climate knowledge, to climate justice policy, and lived experience. The climate leaders developed a framework that centers on supporting hyper-local environmental justice and climate resilience, knowledge exchange, and strengthening the “R” (resilience) in SPARCC.

Microgrants were offered to support local projects and policy approaches and speak to the themes of environmental justice, climate resilience, energy efficiency, green infrastructure, and preventing climate displacement - as it aligns with SPARCC’s platform.. In Memphis, for example, urban gardening and soil health was supported and further integrated to table priorities.

By providing a platform for knowledge exchange and coordination, partners have uplifted work on community resilience hubs, which is a priority across the network. Partners also uplifted resilience through a collaborative response to the Department of Energy’s RFI on Inclusive Innovation and Entrepreneurship in Climate Technology.

Federal Partner Engagement

The Climate Leaders Network in SPARCC offers a model of the integration of climate and environmental justice and equitable community development at a pivotal moment. As federal infrastructure funding is being deployed, there is a need to ensure that climate and environmental justice leaders are poised to receive and implement the dollars to address historic environmental racism. Community development partners and financial institutions must also be poised to deeply engage with these partners and issues - much more deeply than before. Some of our climate leaders, like the West Atlanta Watershed Alliance, are engaging more deeply in capital development, bringing experience and applied learning to this area. SPARCC Climate Leaders is well-suited to bring issues and opportunities to federal agencies, like EPA, to share applied learning, uplift the historical context of structural barriers to implementation stemming from environmental exploitation, and demonstrate models of what is possible in communities. The Climate Leaders group has co-created an agenda to engage with EPA partners to strengthen racial equity in policies, processes, and practices and implement principles and metrics into the programs they administer. The group seeks to foster long term relationship building and identify accountability measures between SPARCC and federal agency staff and leadership.

The basis of our success is the interconnections of our work and experiences. There is a need for all of our collective voices in SPARCC to find common understanding of the challenges of racial inequity, climate injustice, and health disparities. Doing so will build new connections and new ideas. The opportunity is ripe to help others in decision making roles to make these connections so that our homes and communities can be more resilient and just.
